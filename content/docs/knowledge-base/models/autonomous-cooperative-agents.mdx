## Overview

An **autonomous cooperative agent** (or "cooperate-bot") is a proposed design pattern in which a person delegates a recurring budget (\$200-500/month) to an AI agent whose mandate is to maximize the person's cooperative standing. The agent allocates funds through reciprocal gestures, public goods contributions, and professional relationship maintenance — handling the coordination overhead that makes real-world cooperation expensive even when people are willing.

The concept addresses a specific bottleneck: cooperation often fails not because of unwillingness but because of **attention and coordination costs**. People forget who helped them, don't know what others currently need, and find that small reciprocal gestures (\$5-50) aren't worth the cognitive overhead. An agent with a budget could solve this coordination tax.

However, critical analysis reveals several severe failure modes — including gaming, transactionalization of genuine relationships, surveillance requirements, and wealth-based stratification — that constrain viable implementations to narrower, more supervised designs than the theoretical ideal.

### Allocation Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Gaming Risk | Example |
|----------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| **Reciprocity engine** | Track incoming help, reciprocate proportionally | High — cheap "favors" to extract value | Someone shares your work, bot sends a thank-you gift |
| **Public goods contributor** | Fund shared resources the principal's community depends on | Low — projects are verifiable | Monthly contributions to open source dependencies |
| **Social insurance** | Multiple bots pool resources for members facing sudden needs | Medium — moral hazard | Bot-to-bot mutual aid network |
| **Proactive favor bank** | Offer help to network members before being asked | High — hard to verify value | "Principal's bot is offering to cover first \$100 of your design work" |
| **Credible commitment** | Pre-committed funds with public, auditable allocation rules | Low — rules are transparent | Escrow that proves cooperation is reliable |

- Charitable donations to 501(c)(3)s are potentially deductible even when bot-selected, since the principal authorized the agent and it's their money (untested legally)
- If multiple people pool money via bot-to-bot coordination, the structure may resemble an investment club or informal fund, requiring legal structure
- At \$5k+/month or with pooling across principals, legal consultation is needed

## Game-Theoretic Foundations

The cooperate-bot concept draws on several well-established results:

### Coordination Cost as Cooperation Barrier

The standard framing of cooperation failures emphasizes *incentive problems* (prisoner's dilemma, free-riding). But in many real-world settings, people are willing to cooperate — the bottleneck is **coordination cost**. Finding the right person to help, timing the help correctly, and tracking reciprocal obligations all have transaction costs that dwarf the value of small cooperative acts.

An agent that eliminates coordination costs changes the game-theoretic structure: actions that were negative expected value (cooperation cost > cooperation benefit) become positive. This is analogous to how payment processors made micropayments viable by reducing transaction costs below the value of small transactions.

### Credible Commitment

In repeated games, cooperation is sustainable when agents can credibly commit to future actions.[^1] A cooperate-bot with escrowed funds and public allocation rules is a **commitment device**: others can verify that "if I help this person, their agent *will* reciprocate" — removing the trust uncertainty that causes cooperation to unravel.

This converts a repeated game with incomplete information (will they reciprocate?) into one with verifiable commitments (the bot's rules are auditable). The theoretical cooperation-sustaining effect is well-established; the open question is whether it survives gaming in practice.

[^1]: Axelrod, R. (1984). The Evolution of Cooperation. Basic Books. The "tit-for-tat" strategy wins iterated prisoner's dilemma tournaments by being cooperative, retaliatory, forgiving, and transparent — properties a well-designed cooperate-bot would replicate.

### Connection to CAUMF Proposals

Contribution-Adjusted Utility Maximization Funds (CAUMFs) propose pooling donor money into a fund that maximizes each donor's individual preferences, weighted by contribution size.[^2] The theoretical example: two agents with different utility functions across four interventions achieve 33% more combined utility by pooling versus acting alone.

A cooperate-bot is effectively a **single-person CAUMF** — one donor, one autonomous allocator. The interesting extension is that multiple cooperate-bots could negotiate bilateral coordination trades, achieving the same pooling gains without needing a central fund manager or complex legal structure.

[^2]: Ozzie Gooen (2023). ["Contribution-Adjusted Utility Maximization Funds."](https://forum.effectivealtruism.org/posts/Fit37Gs7pwNau7di9/contribution-adjusted-utility-maximization-funds-an-early) Effective Altruism Forum.

| Feature | CAUMF | Cooperate-Bot | Comparison |
|---------|-------|---------------|------------|
| Coordination gains | High (many donors) | Low until bots network | CAUMF wins |
| Preference fidelity | Low (manager estimates) | High (long-term relationship) | Bot wins |
| Overhead | 5-20% management cost | Near-zero once built | Bot wins |
| Legal complexity | Very high (returning money) | Low (personal spending) | Bot wins |
| Gaming resistance | Medium (human judgment) | Low (automated heuristics) | CAUMF wins |

## Failure Modes

### 1. Gaming and Exploitation

**Severity: High. Probability: Near-certain.**

If the bot's reciprocation heuristics are known (or even approximately known), people will optimize against them rather than genuinely cooperate. Crafting low-cost "helpful" interactions that trigger bot reciprocation is the rational strategy for anyone who discovers the system.

**Concrete attack:** Generate AI-written "thoughtful" weekly emails to 20 people with cooperate-bots. Each bot allocates \$25/month in reciprocation. Cost: 10 minutes/week. Revenue: \$500/month. This attack is cheap, scalable, and hard to distinguish from genuine helpfulness.

**Why it's hard to solve:** The fundamental difficulty is measuring the *cost to the giver* rather than the *frequency of giving*. A friend who privately defends your reputation in one difficult meeting is cooperating more than someone who shares your blog post daily, but the blog shares are measurable and the private defense is not.

### 2. Transactionalization of Relationships

**Severity: High. Probability: High in personal contexts.**

Genuine cooperation is partly valuable *because* it is not transactional. Introducing financial reciprocation changes the social meaning of cooperative acts. Behavioral economics research on motivation crowding demonstrates this clearly: introducing financial incentives for blood donation *reduces* donations, because it shifts the act from gift to transaction.[^3]

[^3]: Titmuss, R. (1970). The Gift Relationship: From Human Blood to Social Policy. Allen and Unwin. Demonstrated that commodifying blood donation reduced supply by crowding out altruistic motivation.

**Predicted dynamics once a cooperate-bot exists:**
- Recipients wonder whether help was genuine or bot-optimized
- Helpers calculate ROI of assisting the principal
- Relationships shift from gift economy to market economy
- The cooperation signal (which carried information about genuine care) is destroyed

**Key constraint:** The cooperate-bot likely only works for **arms-length professional cooperation** — open source contributions, professional reciprocity, public goods — not personal relationships where the gift-economy frame matters.

### 3. Surveillance Requirements

**Severity: Medium-High. Probability: Certain if automated.**

To know who has helped the principal, the bot needs access to email, calendar, social media, financial records, and messages. Even if the principal consents, the people interacting with them have not consented to being scored by an automated cooperation tracker.

**The privacy paradox:** Better social context understanding produces better allocations, but requires more invasive monitoring. Manual logging ("Alice helped me today") preserves privacy but defeats the automation value proposition.

### 4. Goodhart's Law on Cooperation Metrics

**Severity: Medium-High. Probability: High.**

The bot optimizes for measurable cooperation proxies. These will diverge from actual cooperation.

| Measurable (Over-Rewarded) | Unmeasurable (Under-Rewarded) |
|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Commits to your open source repo | Privately defending your reputation |
| Social media mentions of your work | Choosing *not* to compete with you for a grant |
| Emails with useful-looking content | Giving honest critical feedback that hurts |
| Event attendance | Redirecting talent/opportunities to you quietly |

Over time, the bot selects for **performative cooperators** — people who are good at generating visible cooperation signals rather than people who genuinely help.

### 5. Wealth Stratification

**Severity: Medium. Probability: Certain in networked version.**

A \$500/month cooperate-bot means the principal's cooperation is "worth" \$500/month. A wealthy person's \$50k/month bot becomes the most "cooperative" node in any network — not through genuine cooperation but through spending power.

In bot-to-bot negotiation, low-budget bots are systematically outmatched. The system replicates existing power dynamics rather than enabling cooperation based on genuine mutual aid.

### 6. Commitment Ratchet

**Severity: Medium. Probability: Likely.**

The bot makes recurring cooperative commitments. These accumulate: monthly donations to 15 projects, regular tips to 10 people, standing agreements with 5 other bots. Total committed: \$480/month. When a new opportunity arises, no budget remains. Reducing the budget means reneging on commitments, damaging cooperative reputation.

This is the cooperate-bot equivalent of subscription creep.

### 7. Collusion in Bot Networks

**Severity: Medium. Probability: Likely if bot-to-bot negotiation exists.**

When bots negotiate with each other, they can form coalitions that exclude outsiders. Ten cooperate-bots discover they achieve higher combined utility by cooperating exclusively with each other, forming an implicit cartel. Outsiders without bots cannot participate.

This mirrors how pooled political spending optimizes for insiders' interests while technically following cooperation rules.

## Viable V1 Design

Given the failure modes above, a viable first version is substantially more constrained than the theoretical ideal:

| Parameter | V1 Constraint | Rationale |
|-----------|--------------|-----------|
| Budget | \$200-500/month | Low enough that failures are affordable |
| Autonomy | Bot *proposes*, human *approves* weekly | Prevents gaming and bad allocations |
| Scope | Public goods and professional reciprocity only | Avoids transactionalization of personal relationships |
| Networking | No bot-to-bot negotiation | Too many attack surfaces |
| Transparency | Public category breakdown, not specific recipients | Balance of accountability and privacy |
| Commitments | All recurring allocations expire in 30 days | Prevents commitment ratchet |
| Kill switch | Instant pause, 30-day wind-down for commitments | Principal retains full control |

**Allocation categories for V1:**

1. **Open source dependencies** — automated, low gaming risk. Fund projects the principal actually uses.
2. **Creator/researcher support** — semi-automated. Subscribe to or tip people whose work the principal reads.
3. **Professional reciprocity** — human-triggered. "Alice helped me with X — allocate \$Y." The bot handles logistics, the human provides judgment.

**Critical design choice:** The V1 is essentially a **smart allocation assistant with a budget**, not an autonomous economic agent. The human provides the cooperation judgment; the bot handles the friction of actually executing on it. This is less exciting than the full vision but avoids most failure modes.

## Open Questions

1. **Is the constrained V1 useful enough?** If the human must approve allocations weekly, the coordination cost savings may be too small to justify the system. The value proposition depends on the bot being noticeably better than the human at identifying *where* cooperation spending produces the most value — and it's unclear an AI can be, given how much cooperation value is illegible.

2. **Can gaming resistance improve over time?** If the bot accumulates data on which allocations actually produced cooperative returns (the helper continued helping, the project stayed maintained), it could learn to distinguish genuine from performative cooperation. But this requires long time horizons and good outcome measurement.

3. **Does the commitment device argument hold?** The strongest theoretical case is not "a bot that spends your money" but "a credible, public commitment to cooperate backed by escrowed funds." This might be valuable even if the allocation quality is mediocre — the signal itself changes others' behavior.

4. **What happens when these become common?** If 10% of knowledge workers have cooperate-bots, does a new cooperative equilibrium emerge (everyone is better off) or a new arms race (you need a bot to keep up)?

5. **Legal structure for bot-to-bot pooling.** If V2+ involves bots coordinating across principals, what legal entity holds the pooled funds? Existing structures (DAOs, investment clubs, donor-advised funds) all have friction that may negate the coordination gains.
