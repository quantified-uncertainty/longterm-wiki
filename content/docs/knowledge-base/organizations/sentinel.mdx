## Overview

**Sentinel** is a foresight and emergency response organization focused on anticipating and mitigating large-scale catastrophes, with particular attention to tail-risk scenarios.[^1] Founded by <EntityLink id="nuno-sempere">Nuño Sempere</EntityLink> in the early 2020s, Sentinel combines automated intelligence gathering with human probabilistic judgment: language models process large volumes of news items weekly for initial triage, and the most credible signals are then routed to skilled forecasters who assign calibrated probabilities to emerging threats.

Sentinel grew out of Sempere's work with <EntityLink id="samotsvety">Samotsvety Forecasting</EntityLink>, one of the higher-accuracy superforecasting groups associated with the effective altruism community, and carries forward that group's emphasis on quantified risk assessment. Its core architecture has two stages: high-throughput machine scanning for weak signals, followed by deliberative probabilistic reasoning from domain-aware forecasters. This separates Sentinel from both purely algorithmic monitoring services and conventional research organizations, neither of which routinely pairs broad surveillance with calibrated probability estimates on tail risks. The intended output is actionable early warnings expressed as confidence intervals rather than qualitative assessments.

## Core Activities

### Risk Detection Pipeline

Sentinel operates a structured, multi-stage pipeline for identifying and assessing global catastrophic risks. Automated ingestion draws on millions of news items, academic preprints, policy documents, and social media signals each week across dozens of languages and regions. <EntityLink id="E400" name="large-language-models">Large Language Models</EntityLink> filter this volume down to a prioritized shortlist, flagging items against predefined criteria — scale, novelty, irreversibility, and escalation potential. Human forecasters with demonstrated calibration track records then evaluate each flagged item, assign probability estimates grounded in reference-class reasoning, and situate developments within longer-term risk trajectories. Final outputs are packaged into structured briefs distributed on a weekly cadence.

### Weekly Briefs

Each weekly brief is organized around a consistent set of risk categories, enabling readers to track developments across domains without re-orienting each issue:

| Category | Scope |
|---|---|
| **AI risks** | Capability jumps, alignment failures, governance breakdowns |
| **Geopolitical threats** | Interstate conflicts, nuclear escalation, gray-zone incidents |
| **Biosecurity** | Engineered or naturally emerging pandemic threats |
| **Emerging catastrophes** | Novel or poorly precedented risks lacking established monitoring frameworks |
| **Probability updates** | Quantitative shifts in forecasted likelihoods for tracked scenarios |

Central to each brief is the **risk status indicator** — a color-coded ordinal signal consolidating the forecasting team's aggregate view of near-term catastrophic risk into a single at-a-glance readout. The indicator surfaces directional changes week over week, functioning as a running thermometer for civilizational-scale threat levels. Significant movements are accompanied by brief explanatory notes attributing shifts to specific developments in the underlying risk categories.

## Media and Distribution

### Newsletter

Sentinel publishes via Substack with free and paid subscription tiers. Free subscribers receive regular risk briefings and analytical summaries; paid members gain access to community Slack channels and directly support the organization's detection work.[^1]

### Sentinel Minutes Podcast

*Sentinel Minutes* distills key findings into concise audio episodes available on Apple Podcasts, Spotify, and RSS. The format extends Sentinel's reach to professionals who engage with risk intelligence in contexts where reading is impractical.

| Channel | Format | Access | Primary Audience |
|---|---|---|---|
| Substack Newsletter | Long-form written analysis | Free & Paid tiers | Researchers, policymakers, general public |
| Community Slack | Discussion & updates | Paid subscribers only | Engaged supporters & analysts |
| Sentinel Minutes Podcast | Audio summaries | Free (Apple, Spotify, RSS) | Professionals, audio-preference listeners |

## Relationship to AI Safety

Sentinel's work intersects with AI safety in two distinct ways.

**AI as a monitored risk category**: Sentinel treats advanced AI as one of several catastrophic risk domains under active surveillance, assigning probabilities to AI-related catastrophe scenarios to produce structured, auditable signals for a field that often relies on qualitative expert judgment.

**AI as an operational tool**: Sentinel uses large language models within its detection and summarization pipeline, making it a live case study in AI-assisted risk assessment. This dual dependency — AI as both subject and instrument — raises questions about reliability and circularity that the organization treats as ongoing methodological concerns.

| Dimension | Role of AI at Sentinel |
|---|---|
| Risk object | AI developments monitored as potential catastrophic hazards |
| Pipeline tool | LLMs used for signal detection and report summarization |
| Governance input | Forecast outputs shared with policy-oriented research communities |

Team members are active in effective altruism and rationalist communities where AI safety is a central concern, creating overlap with organizations such as [MIRI](https://intelligence.org), [Redwood Research](https://www.redwoodresearch.org), and the broader technical AI safety ecosystem. This embedding facilitates cross-pollination around threat modeling and forecasting epistemics.

The resulting position is unusual: Sentinel simultaneously treats AI as an object of concern warranting systematic surveillance and as an operational resource whose outputs inform that same surveillance — distinguishing it from organizations focused exclusively on either AI safety research or general catastrophic risk monitoring.

## Key Uncertainties

Several unresolved questions bear directly on Sentinel's long-term credibility and practical usefulness.

**Detection pipeline efficacy.** AI-assisted monitoring may perform well on risk patterns resembling historical precedents but face structural challenges with genuinely novel threats — precisely the scenarios Sentinel prioritizes. A system that fails silently on unprecedented events offers false assurance rather than genuine foresight.

**Signal calibration.** The tradeoff between false positives (alarm fatigue) and false negatives (missed catastrophic warnings) accumulates into reputational and institutional consequences over time. Sentinel has not publicly specified its operating thresholds or the criteria by which they are adjusted.

**Comparative validation.** Without systematic benchmarking against established intelligence, geopolitical, or security organizations, it is difficult to determine whether Sentinel's assessments provide marginal value or largely duplicate existing work.

**Forecaster network quality.** Expanding the forecaster pool risks diluting the epistemic norms — calibration, scope sensitivity, and independence — that give probabilistic risk estimates their credibility.

**Funding sustainability.** Organizations focused on speculative long-run risks face persistent pressure to demonstrate near-term legible impact, which can gradually distort research priorities toward more tractable but less novel questions.

**Evidential standards.** The field lacks consensus on what would constitute proof that anticipatory catastrophic-risk forecasting is valuable, making it difficult to evaluate Sentinel's success or attract sustained institutional support.
