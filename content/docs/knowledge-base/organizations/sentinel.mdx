## Overview

**Sentinel** is a foresight and emergency response organization dedicated to anticipating and mitigating large-scale catastrophes, with a particular emphasis on speculative and tail-risk scenarios.[^1] Founded by <EntityLink id="nuno-sempere">Nuño Sempere</EntityLink> in the early 2020s, Sentinel operates at the intersection of automated intelligence gathering and elite human judgment: it processes millions of news items weekly using language models for initial triage, then routes the most credible signals to skilled forecasters who assign calibrated probabilities to emerging threats.

The organization grew directly out of Sempere's involvement with <EntityLink id="samotsvety">Samotsvety Forecasting</EntityLink>, one of the most accurate superforecasting groups in the effective altruism ecosystem, and inherits that community's commitment to rigorous, quantified risk assessment. What distinguishes Sentinel from conventional think tanks or automated monitoring services is its two-stage architecture: machine-speed scanning for weak signals, followed by deliberative probabilistic reasoning from domain-aware forecasters. This design targets a specific gap — neither purely algorithmic systems nor traditional research organizations routinely combine high-throughput surveillance with calibrated probability estimates on tail risks. Sentinel's output is intended to give decision-makers an early warning layer with actionable confidence intervals rather than qualitative assessments alone.

## Core Activities

### Risk Detection Pipeline

Sentinel operates a structured, multi-stage pipeline for identifying and assessing global catastrophic risks. The process begins with automated ingestion, drawing on millions of news items, academic preprints, policy documents, and social media signals each week from sources spanning dozens of languages and regions. Large language models then filter this raw volume down to a prioritized shortlist, flagging items that meet predefined criteria related to scale, novelty, irreversibility, or escalation potential. Human forecasters — recruited from elite forecasting communities with demonstrated calibration track records — evaluate each flagged item, assign probability estimates grounded in reference-class reasoning, and situate individual developments within longer-term risk trajectories. Final outputs are packaged into structured briefs and distributed on a weekly cadence.

1. **Automated Collection**: Ingesting millions of news items, preprints, and policy documents weekly across multiple languages and source types
2. **AI Prioritization**: Language models filter and rank candidate events against a standing set of catastrophic-risk criteria, including scale, novelty, and escalation potential
3. **Forecaster Assessment**: Elite human forecasters evaluate flagged items, assign calibrated probability estimates, and update trend lines for tracked scenarios
4. **Publication**: Curated weekly summaries distributed via newsletter and podcast

### Weekly Briefs

Each weekly brief is organized around a consistent set of risk categories, enabling readers to track developments across domains without re-orienting each issue:

- **AI risks**: Capability jumps, alignment failures, and governance breakdowns in artificial intelligence
- **Geopolitical threats**: Interstate conflicts, nuclear escalation scenarios, and gray-zone incidents
- **Biosecurity**: Engineered or naturally emerging pandemic threats
- **Emerging catastrophes**: Novel or poorly precedented risks lacking established monitoring frameworks
- **Probability updates**: Quantitative shifts in forecasted likelihoods for tracked scenarios

Central to each brief is the **risk status indicator** — an ordinal or color-coded signal that consolidates the forecasting team's aggregate view of near-term catastrophic risk into a single at-a-glance readout. Rather than requiring readers to parse full probability tables, the indicator surfaces directional changes week over week, functioning as a running thermometer for civilizational-scale threat levels. Significant movements in the indicator are accompanied by brief explanatory notes attributing the shift to specific developments in the underlying risk categories.

## Media and Distribution

### Newsletter

Sentinel publishes via Substack, offering both free and paid subscription tiers. The free tier provides access to regular risk briefings and analytical summaries, making core content available to a broad audience that includes researchers, journalists, and policymakers. Paid members gain access to community Slack channels and provide direct financial support for the organization's detection work.[^1] The Substack format enables Sentinel to reach readers who prefer long-form written analysis delivered directly to their inboxes, lowering the barrier to engaging with complex catastrophic-risk content.

### Sentinel Minutes Podcast

*Sentinel Minutes* is a podcast that distills key findings and risk assessments into concise audio summaries, available on Apple Podcasts, Spotify, and RSS feeds. The format serves professionals who monitor catastrophic risk during commutes or in other contexts where reading is impractical. By translating written analyses into accessible audio episodes, Sentinel broadens its reach beyond the newsletter's core readership and engages listeners who might not otherwise encounter long-form risk intelligence.

| Channel | Format | Access | Primary Audience |

## Relationship to AI Safety

Sentinel's work intersects with AI safety in two distinct and sometimes tension-producing ways.

**AI as a monitored risk category**: Sentinel treats advanced AI as one of several catastrophic risk domains under active surveillance. This positions the organization within the broader AI governance discourse, where forecasting-based early warning systems are increasingly recognized as a mechanism for triggering policy responses before risks fully materialize. By assigning probabilities to AI-related catastrophe scenarios, Sentinel contributes structured, auditable signals to a field that often relies on qualitative expert judgment.

**AI as an operational tool**: Sentinel uses large language models within its detection and summarization pipeline, making it a live case study in AI-assisted risk assessment. This dual dependency — on AI as both subject and instrument — introduces non-trivial questions about reliability, circularity, and alignment that the organization has acknowledged as ongoing methodological concerns.

| Dimension | Role of AI at Sentinel |
|---|---|
| Risk object | AI developments monitored as potential catastrophic hazards |
| Pipeline tool | LLMs used for signal detection and report summarization |
| Governance input | Forecast outputs shared with EA/rationalist policy networks |

Team members are active in effective altruism and rationalist communities where AI safety is a central concern, creating meaningful overlap with organizations such as [MIRI](https://intelligence.org), [Redwood Research](https://www.redwoodresearch.org), and the broader technical AI safety ecosystem. This community embedding facilitates methodological cross-pollination — particularly around threat modeling and forecasting epistemics — as well as shared dissemination of early-warning outputs to audiences already oriented toward long-horizon risk.

The resulting position is unusual: Sentinel simultaneously treats AI as an object of concern warranting systematic surveillance and as an operational resource whose outputs inform that same surveillance. Navigating this duality is an ongoing institutional challenge that distinguishes Sentinel from organizations focused exclusively on either AI safety research or general catastrophic risk monitoring.

## Key Uncertainties

Several unresolved questions bear directly on Sentinel's long-term credibility and practical usefulness, and honest assessment requires engaging with them directly.

**Detection pipeline efficacy.** AI-assisted monitoring may perform well on risk patterns resembling historical precedents but face structural challenges with genuinely novel threats — precisely the scenarios Sentinel prioritizes. Whether the system can detect distributional shift in its inputs, and flag when a situation falls outside its effective domain, remains unclear. A system that fails silently on unprecedented events offers false assurance rather than genuine foresight.

**Signal calibration.** The tradeoff between false positives (alarm fatigue eroding stakeholder trust) and false negatives (missed catastrophic warnings) is not purely technical — it accumulates into reputational and institutional consequences over time. Sentinel has not publicly specified its operating thresholds or the criteria by which they are adjusted.

**Comparative validation.** Without systematic benchmarking against outputs from established intelligence, geopolitical, or security organizations, it is difficult to determine whether Sentinel's assessments provide marginal value or largely duplicate existing work at greater cost.

**Scalability and forecaster quality.** Expanding the forecaster network risks diluting the epistemic norms — calibration, scope sensitivity, and independence — that give probabilistic risk estimates their credibility in the first place.

**Funding sustainability.** Organizations focused on speculative long-run risks face persistent pressure to demonstrate near-term legible impact to funders, which can gradually distort research priorities toward more tractable but less novel questions.

**Evidential standards.** The broader field lacks consensus on what would constitute proof that anticipatory catastrophic-risk forecasting is valuable, making it difficult to evaluate Sentinel's success or attract sustained institutional support.
