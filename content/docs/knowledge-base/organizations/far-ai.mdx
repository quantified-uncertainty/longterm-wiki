## Overview

<EntityLink id="E138" name="far-ai">FAR AI</EntityLink> ([far.ai](https://www.far.ai/)) is an AI safety research nonprofit founded in July 2022 by Adam Gleave (CEO) and Karl Berzins (President).[^rc-1b93] Based in Berkeley, California, the organization's stated mission is to ensure AI systems are trustworthy and beneficial to society.[^rc-e6d2] FAR AI incorporated in October 2022 as a 501(c)(3) nonprofit (EIN 92-0692207), having initially operated as a fiscally sponsored project.[^rc-24b7]

The organization conducts technical research in <EntityLink id="E1" name="adversarial-robustness">adversarial robustness</EntityLink>, interpretability, model evaluation, and alignment, with a focus on fundamental AI safety challenges described as too large or resource-intensive for academic labs alone.[^rc-18d6] As of early 2026, FAR AI has published 30 research papers, hosted over 1,000 attendees across 10+ events, and operates FAR.Labs, a coworking space in Berkeley with 40+ active members.[^rc-1b93] FAR AI reported \$24.3 million in revenue and \$8.6 million in expenses for fiscal year 2024, and secured over \$30 million in additional funding commitments in 2025.[^rc-e411][^rc-e88c]

Notable outputs include a 2023 paper demonstrating that adversarial policies can defeat the superhuman Go AI KataGo with greater than 77% win rate even when KataGo uses superhuman-level search, and co-authorship of the "Towards Guaranteed Safe AI" framework paper published in May 2024.[^rc-8f49][^rc-18d6] In early 2026, FAR AI was selected by the European Commission's AI Office to lead CBRN (chemical, biological, radiological, and nuclear) risk research.[^rc-e88c]

## Key Research Areas

### Adversarial Robustness

FAR AI's empirical research on <EntityLink id="E1" name="adversarial-robustness">adversarial robustness</EntityLink> has produced several findings concerning the gap between AI capability and reliability. The organization's core results in this area are summarized below:

| Research Focus | Approach | Key Finding | Publications |
|----------------|----------|-------------|--------------|
| Adversarial Attacks on Go AI | Training adversarial policies against KataGo | >77% win rate even with superhuman search; adversarial policies lose to human amateurs | "Adversarial Policies Beat Superhuman Go AIs" (2023)[^rc-8f49] |
| Go AI Defense Analysis | Testing adversarial training, iterated adversarial training, vision transformers | None of three defenses withstood adaptive attacks | "Can Go AIs be adversarially robust?" (2024)[^rc-7d54] |
| LLM <EntityLink id="E583" name="adversarial-training">Adversarial Training</EntityLink> | Adversarial training vs. scaling for robustness | Orders-of-magnitude efficiency gains over scaling alone | FAR AI robustness research[^rc-db17] |
| Multi-layer Defense Bypass | STACK attack method against layered AI defenses | 71% success rate bypassing multi-layered defenses | FAR AI research overview[^rc-e6d2] |
| LLM Data Poisoning | Scale effects on vulnerability | Larger language models more vulnerable; result across 23 LLMs from 8 model series | FAR AI robustness research[^rc-db17] |

A landmark 2023 paper, "Adversarial Policies Beat Superhuman Go AIs," demonstrated that adversarial policies achieved a >99% win rate against KataGo when it uses no tree-search, and a >77% win rate even when KataGo uses superhuman-level search.[^rc-8f49] The adversarial policies win by inducing blunders in KataGo rather than by playing stronger Go, and are themselves easily beaten by human amateur players.[^rc-8f49] A follow-up 2024 paper, "Can Go AIs be adversarially robust?," tested three natural defenses—positional adversarial training, iterated adversarial training, and vision transformer architectures—and found that none could withstand adaptive adversarial attacks.[^rc-7d54]

FAR AI has also found that adversarial training improves language model robustness orders of magnitude more efficiently than scaling model size alone, and that larger language models are more vulnerable to data poisoning—a result demonstrated across 23 LLMs from 8 model series.[^rc-db17] The organization's STACK attack method achieved a 71% success rate bypassing multi-layered AI defenses, identifying gaps in defense-in-depth strategies.[^rc-e6d2]

### LLM Red-Teaming and Model Evaluation

FAR AI began red-teaming leading language models for frontier labs in Q4 2023, including red-teaming of GPT-4.[^rc-1b93] The organization was selected by the European Commission's AI Office to lead CBRN risk research, reflecting its expanding role in frontier model evaluation.[^rc-e88c] Research is delivered through peer-reviewed publications, governmental partnerships, and red-teaming engagements.[^rc-e6d2]

### <EntityLink id="E213" name="natural-abstractions">Natural Abstractions</EntityLink> Research

FAR AI has expressed theoretical interest in <EntityLink id="E213" name="natural-abstractions">natural abstractions</EntityLink> research, which explores whether intelligent systems independently converge on similar conceptual representations of the world. The research questions in this area include whether intelligent systems discover the same abstractions, whether neural networks learn natural abstractions (with implications for the foundations of <EntityLink id="E174" name="interpretability">interpretability</EntityLink>), and whether shared conceptual bases could enable verification of alignment between human and AI cognition. This direction connects to work by <EntityLink id="E202" name="miri">MIRI</EntityLink> and other organizations investigating whether shared abstractions could provide a foundation for alignment approaches. The natural abstractions hypothesis has theoretical appeal but, as of early 2026, requires extensive empirical validation across diverse AI architectures and training regimes.

| Research Question | Hypothesis | Implications | Status |
|-------------------|------------|--------------|--------|
| Universal Concepts | Intelligent systems discover same abstractions | Shared conceptual basis for alignment | Theoretical development |
| Neural Network Learning | Do NNs learn natural abstractions? | <EntityLink id="E174" name="interpretability">Interpretability</EntityLink> foundations | Empirical investigation |
| Alignment Verification | Can we verify shared concepts? | Communication with AI systems | Early research |
| Mathematical Universality | Math/physics as natural abstractions | Foundation for value alignment | Ongoing |

## Organizational Structure and Operations

### Leadership

FAR AI was founded in July 2022 by Adam Gleave and Karl Berzins.[^rc-1b93] Gleave serves as Co-founder & CEO, while Berzins serves as Co-founder & President.[^rc-8f49] Gleave completed his PhD in artificial intelligence at UC Berkeley under <EntityLink id="E290" name="stuart-russell">Stuart Russell</EntityLink>'s supervision; his research focuses on developing techniques for AI systems to act according to human preferences.[^rc-7fa2] According to public Form 990 filings, Gleave's compensation was \$229,331 and Berzins's was \$182,641 in fiscal year 2024.[^rc-e411]

### Organizational Structure

FAR AI is incorporated as a 501(c)(3) nonprofit with EIN 92-0692207, tax-exempt since November 2020.[^rc-e411] The organization maintains a policy capping revenue from for-profit AI developers at a maximum of 10% of total annual revenue, and charges market rates for consulting engagements to avoid subsidizing private actors.[^rc-24b7] FAR AI commits to disclosing the fraction of revenue derived from such consulting.[^rc-24b7] FAR AI reported \$24.3 million in revenue and \$8.6 million in expenses for fiscal year 2024, per its Form 990 filed November 14, 2025.[^rc-e411]

### Research Team

Public filings and organizational materials cite varying staff counts at different points in the organization's growth. By late 2023, FAR AI had scaled to 12 full-time staff and had produced 13 academic papers.[^rc-0a39] More recent organizational materials reference at least 17–18 technical staff members alongside dedicated operations and communications personnel.[^rc-8f49][^rc-e411] The organization maintains Operations, Communications, and Technical Staff departments.

### FAR.Labs Co-working Space

FAR.Labs is a co-working hub located in downtown Berkeley, opened in March 2023, and houses 40+ active members working on AI safety problems.[^rc-0a39] It situates FAR AI within the Berkeley AI safety ecosystem alongside <EntityLink id="E57" name="chai">CHAI</EntityLink> and other research groups.

## Current State & Trajectory

FAR AI was incorporated in October 2022 and has grown rapidly since founding.[^rc-24b7] By late 2023, the organization had scaled to 12 full-time staff and produced 13 academic papers.[^rc-0a39] As of early 2026, FAR AI has published 30 research papers across robustness, value alignment, and model evaluation, and fields at least 17 technical staff members.[^rc-e411]

Financially, the organization has expanded substantially. FAR AI reported \$24.3 million in revenue and \$8.6 million in expenses in fiscal year 2024.[^rc-e411] In 2025, FAR AI secured over \$30 million in additional funding commitments to scale its frontier AI safety research.[^rc-e88c] The grantmaking program, launched in Q3 2024 with a \$12 million grant from <EntityLink id="E552" name="open-philanthropy">Open Philanthropy</EntityLink>, extends the organization's reach beyond direct research into supporting the broader field.[^rc-6b84]

Policy engagement has also expanded. FAR AI launched the inaugural Technical Innovations for AI Policy Conference in Washington, D.C. on May 31–June 1, 2025, convening over 150 technical experts, researchers, and policymakers.[^rc-6b84] In early 2026, FAR AI was selected by the European Commission's AI Office to lead CBRN risk research, signaling growing institutional recognition.[^rc-e88c]

The organization's trajectory—from a two-person founding team in 2022 to a multi-program institute with \$24 million or more in annual revenue—reflects a deliberate expansion from research incubator toward a broader role in the technical AI safety ecosystem.[^rc-24b7]

## Research Questions and Uncertainties

### Theoretical Questions

Several theoretical questions shape FAR AI's research direction:

- **Natural Abstractions Validity**: Whether intelligent systems independently converge on similar conceptual representations remains an open empirical question. The <EntityLink id="E213" name="natural-abstractions">natural abstractions</EntityLink> hypothesis has theoretical appeal but requires extensive empirical validation across diverse AI architectures and training regimes.

- **Robustness-Alignment Connection**: The relationship between <EntityLink id="E1" name="adversarial-robustness">adversarial robustness</EntityLink> and value alignment is not fully understood. While robustness may be necessary for aligned systems, the degree to which robustness research directly contributes to solving alignment problems is debated within the AI safety community.

- **Scaling Dynamics**: Whether current robustness and evaluation approaches will remain relevant as AI systems increase in capability is uncertain. Some safety researchers argue that qualitatively new challenges emerge at higher capability levels that may not be addressed by current methodologies.

### Organizational Uncertainties

- **Research Timeline**: Academic publication timelines typically span months to years, including peer review, revision, and conference scheduling. Whether this research pace adequately matches the urgency of safety concerns depends on assessments of timelines for <EntityLink id="E357" name="transformative-ai">Transformative AI</EntityLink> development.

- **Scope Evolution**: FAR AI's research focus may evolve as the field develops. The organization's emphasis on empirical robustness could shift toward other safety approaches depending on which problems prove most tractable or urgent.

- **Policy Engagement**: The extent of FAR AI's involvement in AI governance and policy discussions may expand beyond its current focus on technical research and convening activities.

## Criticisms and Responses

### Academic Pace Concerns

**Criticism**: Academic publication processes operate on timelines of months to years, including peer review, revision, and conference scheduling. Critics argue this pace may be too slow given rapid AI capability advances and the urgency of safety concerns.

**Response**: Proponents of the peer-reviewed publication model argue it ensures research quality and credibility, and that methodology and evaluation frameworks developed through careful research provide lasting value even as specific techniques evolve. Preprint sharing and direct collaboration with AI labs can accelerate impact for time-sensitive findings.

**Context**: This tension between research rigor and speed affects the broader AI safety field. Different organizations make different tradeoffs between publication quality, speed, and direct industry impact.

### Limited Scope Questions

**Criticism**: Research on <EntityLink id="E1" name="adversarial-robustness">adversarial robustness</EntityLink> and evaluation may not directly address core alignment challenges like <EntityLink id="E93" name="deceptive-alignment">deceptive alignment</EntityLink>, goal specification, or value learning. Critics question whether robustness research provides sufficient traction on harder alignment problems. A pointed version of this concern is that demonstrating vulnerabilities in narrow systems—such as the finding that superhuman Go AIs can be beaten by adversarial policies playing cyclic patterns that amateur humans easily defeat[^rc-1b93]—illuminates failure modes without providing a clear path to preventing them in more capable systems.[^rc-24b7]

**Response**: FAR AI argues robustness is a necessary foundation for aligned systems, noting that "most alignment proposals using helper ML systems will fail if helpers are exploited by main systems."[^rc-e411] FAR AI's portfolio also includes value alignment work that produced more sample-efficient value learning algorithms[^rc-64f4] and contributed to the "Towards Guaranteed Safe AI" framework co-authored with researchers including <EntityLink id="E380" name="yoshua-bengio">Yoshua Bengio</EntityLink> and <EntityLink id="E290" name="stuart-russell">Stuart Russell</EntityLink>.[^rc-18d6]

**Context**: The AI safety field contains diverse views on which research directions are most valuable. Some researchers emphasize near-term robustness and evaluation, while others focus on long-term theoretical alignment challenges. FAR AI's finding that adversarial training improves language model robustness orders of magnitude more efficiently than scaling alone[^rc-24b7] is cited as evidence that empirical robustness work can yield generalizable insights.

### Natural Abstractions Theory Concerns

**Criticism**: The <EntityLink id="E213" name="natural-abstractions">natural abstractions</EntityLink> hypothesis lacks extensive empirical validation. Critics argue that theoretical frameworks should be grounded in experimental evidence before receiving substantial research attention.

**Response**: Proponents argue theoretical frameworks can productively guide empirical research programs, and that the multi-year timeline for validation is appropriate given the scope of the hypothesis.

**Context**: Disagreement about when to invest in theoretical versus empirical work is common in early-stage scientific fields. Different researchers make different judgments about the appropriate balance.
