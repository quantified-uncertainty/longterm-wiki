## Overview

The <EntityLink id="E150" name="geopolitics">Geopolitics</EntityLink> dimension of AI development significantly shapes both capability trajectories and risk profiles. International competition can accelerate development timelines and reduce safety standards, while effective <EntityLink id="international-coordination">international coordination</EntityLink> could enable shared safety measures and governance frameworks.

This page tracks key metrics across:
- **Capability gaps** between major powers
- **Talent flows** and human capital distribution
- **Cooperation mechanisms** and international agreements
- **<EntityLink id="racing-dynamics">Racing dynamics</EntityLink>** suggesting competitive pressure
- **<EntityLink id="ai-governance">Governance</EntityLink> effectiveness** of international institutions
- **Technology <EntityLink id="proliferation">proliferation</EntityLink>** to authoritarian regimes

## 1. US-China AI Capability Gap

### Overall Assessment (2024-2025)

**Current Status**: United States maintains overall lead, but gap is narrowing in specific domains.

#### Investment Comparison

| Metric | United States | China | Ratio | Source |
|--------|--------------|-------|-------|--------|
| **Private AI Investment (2024)** | \$109.1 billion | \$9.3 billion | 12:1 US lead | [^1] |
| **Total VC Funding to AI (2025)** | \$159 billion (79% of global) | ≈\$125 billion total AI investment | ≈1.3:1 US lead | [^7] |
| **Government VC for AI** | Limited direct government VC | \$184 billion to ≈10,000 AI firms (cumulative) | China leads in state funding | [^8] |
| **2025 AI Capital Spending** | Not specified | Up to \$98 billion projected | — | [^9] |
| **AI Compute Infrastructure** | 74% of global high-end AI supercomputing | 14% of global capacity | 5.3:1 US lead | [^10] |

**Key Finding**: The US private sector invests nearly 12x more than China's private sector [^1], but China's government-directed investment significantly narrows the total funding gap [^8]. However, the US maintains overwhelming compute infrastructure dominance (74% vs 14% of global AI supercomputing capacity) [^10].

#### AI Talent Distribution

| Metric | United States | China | Source |
|--------|--------------|-------|--------|
| **Top 2% AI talent currently working** | 57% of global total | 28% of global total | [^5] |
| **Top AI researchers produced (2022)** | 18% of global | 47% of global | [^5] |
| **Top-tier talent at US institutions (origin)** | 37% domestic, 38% Chinese-origin | 90% retention of domestic graduates | [^11] |
| **Graduate retention rate** | 80% of those who studied in US | 90% of those who studied in China | [^5] |
| **PhD salary premium** | \$185K average | \$67K average | [^12] |
| **AI papers annually** | 28,400 | 41,200 | [^1] |
| **AI patents filed (2023)** | ≈30% of global | 69.7% of global | [^1] |

**Key Finding**: The US employs the majority of elite AI talent globally (57%) [^5], but China produces more than twice as many top researchers (47% vs 18%) [^5] and is increasingly retaining them domestically. The \$118K salary premium (US vs China) [^12] reportedly drives a significant share of Chinese AI PhDs to relocate to the US, creating a US talent dependency risk.

#### Compute & Hardware

**US Advantages**:
- Dominance in advanced chip design (<EntityLink id="E693" name="nvidia">NVIDIA</EntityLink>, AMD)
- Leading-edge semiconductor manufacturing access
- Export controls limiting China's access to cutting-edge GPUs

**China Challenges & Strategies**:
- Significant lag in domestic chip fabrication (estimated 2–5 years behind, according to some analysts)
- Huawei developing AI chips to circumvent US restrictions
- Building massive chip clusters with domestic hardware
- Government subsidies for electricity costs at data centers using domestic chips
- Focus on efficiency and open-source models (e.g., DeepSeek)

**CSET Assessment**: According to CSET Georgetown [^13], "China still lags significantly due to constraints imposed by U.S. export restrictions" in semiconductor fabrication, though it is working to close the gap.

#### Model Performance

| Metric | United States | China | Trend | Source |
|--------|--------------|-------|-------|--------|
| **Benchmark performance gap** | Leading | 0.3% behind (2025) | Rapidly closing (was 20% in 2023) | [^1] |
| **Global LLM market share** | 93% of site visits | 13% (up from 3% in 2 months) | China gaining rapidly | [^10] |
| **Model lag estimate** | Leading | 3–6 months behind | Could shift with algorithmic breakthroughs | [^13] |
| **Manufacturing AI adoption** | 45% | 67% | China leads deployment | [^6] |
| **Enterprise software AI** | 71% Fortune 500 adoption | 45% | US leads enterprise | [^14] |

**US Leadership**: Dominates frontier model development (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini) and controls 93% of global LLM market share [^10].

**China Progress**: DeepSeek R1 (January 2025) demonstrated near-parity capabilities; Chinese LLM market share reportedly surged from 3% to 13% in just two months [^10]. China leads in AI deployment scale (67% manufacturing adoption vs 45% in the US) [^6].

**Competitive Dynamic**: While the US focuses on AGI development, China is, according to CSET Georgetown [^13], "outpacing the United States in diffusing AI across its society," though "China has by no means de-emphasized its state-sponsored pursuit of AGI."

## 2. AI Talent Migration Patterns

Data in this section is drawn primarily from the MacroPolo Global AI Talent Tracker (versions 1.0 and 2.0), which analyses researchers publishing at top-tier AI venues. Because no sources are available in the current cache, all specific statistics below are flagged as *reportedly* derived from that tracker and should be verified against the primary source before treating them as authoritative.[^15]

### Key Findings

#### Global Mobility Reportedly Declining

**2019**: Reportedly ~55% of top-tier AI researchers worked outside their home countries.

**2022**: Reportedly ~42% of top-tier AI researchers worked outside their home countries.

**Change**: A reported −13 percentage-point decline, according to some sources suggesting more top-tier talent is staying in home countries rather than migrating.

#### US as Primary Destination

- Reportedly **60%** of top AI institutions globally are located in the United States.
- Reportedly **57%** of the top 2% of global AI talent works in the United States.
- Reportedly **<F e="openai" f="fba50864">75%</F>** of top-tier AI talent at US institutions are of American or Chinese origin (up from a reported 58% in 2019).

#### Composition of US AI Workforce (Top 20%), Reportedly

| Origin | Percentage |
|--------|------------|
| United States | ≈37% |
| China | ≈38% |
| Other | ≈25% |

**Critical Dependency**: According to some sources, the US AI sector is heavily reliant on foreign talent, with Chinese-origin researchers reportedly comprising the single largest group.

#### China's Growing Talent Production & Retention

- **2019**: China reportedly produced ~29% of the world's top AI researchers.
- **2022**: China reportedly produced ~47% of the world's top AI researchers.
- According to some sources, roughly 2,000 AI university majors graduate across China annually.

**Retention**: Reportedly ~90% of researchers who attended graduate school in China subsequently worked in China, with ~28% of top AI researchers globally working in China as of 2022.

**Cited driver**: "The growth of the domestic AI sector in China and the job opportunities it has created."

#### Other Countries

**Gaining Ground**: The United Kingdom, South Korea, and continental Europe have reportedly slightly improved their standing as AI talent destinations.

**Relative Declines**: India and Canada have reportedly seen relative declines as sources of top AI researcher talent.

### Implications

1. **US Vulnerability**: Heavy reported reliance on foreign talent, particularly from strategic competitor China.
2. **China's Trajectory**: Rapidly improving domestic talent pipeline and retention, according to some sources.
3. **Decentralization**: Global AI talent reportedly becoming less concentrated.
4. **Brain-Drain Reversal**: Traditional "brain drain" to the US showing signs of reversal for Chinese-origin talent.

## 3. Multinational AI Cooperation Agreements

### Major Multilateral Initiatives (2024-2025)

| Initiative | Members | Binding? | Enforcement | Key Limitation |
|------------|---------|----------|-------------|----------------|
| **OECD AI Principles** | 47 countries + EU | No | None | Implementation guidance lacking |
| **UNESCO AI Ethics** | 194 member states | No | None | No enforcement mechanism |
| **Council of Europe Convention** | 46 + 11 non-members | Yes (treaty) | Variable by country | Limited ratifications so far |
| **G7 Hiroshima Process** | 7 major democracies | No | None | Excludes China |
| **<EntityLink id="E127" name="eu-ai-act">EU AI Act</EntityLink>** | 27 EU members | Yes | Fines up to €35M or 7% revenue | Only applies to EU market |
| **UN Global AI Resolution** | reportedly 122 co-sponsors | No | None | Normative only |

#### GPAI-OECD Integrated Partnership

**Status**: Launched 2024

**Members**: According to some sources, 44 countries plus the European Union

**Significance**: One of the broader international AI governance partnerships

In December 2024, a GPAI Belgrade Ministerial Declaration was reportedly issued, though full text and signatories should be verified against official GPAI records.

**2024 OECD AI Principles Update**:
- Now endorsed by reportedly **47 jurisdictions** including the EU
- Updated to address general-purpose and generative AI
- Enhanced focus on safety, privacy, intellectual property, and information integrity
- Claims of **87% content overlap** with UNESCO, G7, and UN frameworks have been cited in some analyses but should be treated as indicative rather than precisely verified

#### Council of Europe AI Framework Convention

**Adopted**: According to the Council of Europe, the Framework Convention on Artificial Intelligence was opened for signature on September 5, 2024, following its adoption by the Committee of Ministers on May 17, 2024.[^16]

**Signatories**: The Council of Europe reports that both member states and non-member observer states—including Argentina, Australia, Canada, Israel, Japan, the United States, and the European Union—signed the convention.[^16]

**Nature**: This is described by the Council of Europe as the first binding international legal instrument on AI.[^16]

#### UN Global AI Resolution

**Adopted**: According to the UN, the General Assembly adopted a resolution on "Seizing the opportunities of safe, secure and trustworthy artificial systems for sustainable development" in March 2024.[^17]

**Co-sponsors**: Reportedly co-sponsored by a large number of member states (figures of around 122 have been cited in some sources, but should be confirmed via official UN records).

**Significance**: Widely described as the first global consensus resolution on AI governance.

In December 2024, the UN General Assembly also reportedly adopted a resolution focused on AI in the military domain, stressing the importance of compliance with international humanitarian and human rights law, though this should be verified against official UN documentation.

#### G7 Hiroshima AI Process

**Status**: Ongoing since 2023

**Key Outputs**:
- International Guiding Principles for AI
- Voluntary Code of Conduct for AI developers[^18]

### Bilateral AI Safety & Cooperation Agreements

#### US-UK AI Safety Partnership

**Signed**: April 2024[^19]

**Scope**: Joint development of tests for advanced AI models; aligning scientific approaches to AI safety evaluation; and accelerating development of evaluation suites.[^19]

#### US-Saudi Arabia Strategic AI Partnership

A US-Saudi strategic AI partnership was reportedly announced in 2025. The precise terms and binding status of this arrangement should be confirmed via official government sources, as details remain incompletely documented in public records.

### International Governance Infrastructure

#### OECD AI Policy Observatory

The OECD AI Policy Observatory (oecd.ai) tracks AI policy initiatives across dozens of countries, including national strategies, regulatory frameworks, and sectoral guidelines.[^2] Claims that it monitors over **900 AI policy initiatives** across 69 countries appear in some secondary sources and should be verified directly with the Observatory.

#### UNESCO Implementation

UNESCO has conducted Readiness Assessments for AI ethics implementation with member governments; the figure of **58 governments** participating is cited in some UNESCO communications but should be confirmed against current UNESCO reporting.[^20]

#### African Union Framework (2024)

The African Union adopted an AI policy framework in 2024 with a stated focus on trustworthy and inclusive AI development for the continent.

### Effectiveness Assessment

#### Strengths

1. **Broad Participation**: Large numbers of UN co-sponsors indicate wide nominal buy-in
2. **Convergence**: Multiple analyses suggest significant overlap in principles across major frameworks
3. **Common Language**: OECD definitions are increasingly referenced by the EU, Council of Europe, Japan, and the US, creating a degree of terminological interoperability[^2]

#### Weaknesses

1. **Non-Binding Nature**: Most agreements lack meaningful enforcement mechanisms
2. **Implementation Gap**: The OECD has noted that concrete guidance for implementation is often lacking[^2]
3. **Monitoring Deficit**: Evaluation mechanisms are frequently described as weak, limiting the ability to measure outcomes
4. **Capability-Governance Gap**: A significant gap between AI adoption rates and governance maturity has been observed across multiple national contexts; the specific figure of 53 percentage points cited in some sources cannot be independently verified and should be treated as approximate
5. **Resource Constraints**: Skills shortages, legacy systems, data-sharing difficulties, and financial constraints are widely cited barriers to scaling AI governance

- China reportedly recognizing the need for less-centralized command in AI-era warfare

- Increasing national security rhetoric tied to AI competition

## 5. International AI Governance Body Effectiveness

### Scope of International Governance (2024–2025)

#### National Strategy Proliferation

As of end-2023, approximately **89 national AI strategies** had been documented worldwide, according to the UNCTAD Technology and Innovation Report 2025.[^1] Geographic distribution remains uneven, with coverage concentrated in developed nations while developing countries face reportedly "significant infrastructure and capacity gaps."[^1]

### Major Governance Bodies & Initiatives

#### OECD AI Governance

The OECD AI Policy Observatory tracks more than **900 initiatives across 69 countries**, covering roughly **47 jurisdictions** including the EU.[^2] OECD AI system definitions have reportedly been adopted by the EU, Council of Europe, Japan, and the United States, indicating high cross-jurisdictional interoperability.[^2]

**Strengths**:
- High interoperability across jurisdictions
- Comprehensive policy observatory
- Regular updates to principles

**Limitations**:
- Non-binding nature
- "Flexible and adaptable" principles can mean weak enforcement
- Concrete implementation guidance is often lacking[^2]

#### UNESCO

UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of AI was adopted by all **193 member states**.[^3] According to UNESCO, **58 governments** have since conducted Readiness Assessments evaluating their capacity to implement the Recommendation.[^3]

**Focus**: Ethical frameworks, inclusive development

#### UN AI Initiatives

- **March 2024**: The UN General Assembly passed its first global AI resolution, reportedly with **122 co-sponsors**.[^4]
- **September 2024**: The UN Secretary-General's Office of the Tech Envoy partnered with the OECD on AI governance alignment.[^4]
- **December 2024**: The General Assembly adopted its first resolution specifically addressing AI in the military domain.[^4]

**Limitations**: No enforcement mechanisms; intergovernmental processes are slow relative to AI development pace.

#### G7 Hiroshima AI Process

Participants (US, UK, France, Germany, Italy, Canada, Japan) produced a voluntary code of conduct and guiding principles for advanced AI developers. The Process is regarded as a faster-moving forum than traditional UN bodies, though it excludes China and other significant AI-developing nations.

#### Global Partnership on AI (GPAI)

GPAI merged with the OECD in 2024, consolidating membership of approximately **44 countries** into OECD structures.[^5] Its focus on multistakeholder working groups on key AI challenges continues under the merged arrangement.[^5]

### Effectiveness Metrics

#### Coverage

| Dimension | Rating | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Geographic reach | ✅ Strong | 122–193 countries in various initiatives |
| Policy tracking | ✅ Strong | 900+ initiatives documented[^2] |
| Key player inclusion | ⚠️ Moderate | China participates in some but not all forums |

#### Convergence

According to some analyses, there is reportedly **87% content overlap** across major international AI governance frameworks, suggesting normative convergence even absent formal coordination.[^6] OECD definitions are increasingly adopted across jurisdictions.

#### Implementation

The OECD has noted that "concrete guidance for implementation is often lacking" and that "monitoring and evaluation mechanisms are also weak, limiting the ability to measure outcomes or detect risks effectively."[^2] Some sources report a **53 percentage point gap** between AI adoption rates and governance maturity in many jurisdictions, though this figure should be treated with caution as methodologies vary.[^6]

#### Enforcement

| Dimension | Rating | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Binding frameworks | ❌ Very Weak | Most international frameworks are non-binding |
| Sanctions | ❌ Very Weak | No meaningful sanctions for non-compliance |
| EU exception | ⚠️ Partial | EU AI Act: fines up to €35 million or 7% of global turnover |

#### Speed & Responsiveness

The OECD AI Principles were updated in 2024, approximately five years after their initial 2019 adoption. Traditional intergovernmental processes continue to lag AI development timelines, though forums like the G7 Hiroshima Process operate on accelerated schedules.

### Impact Assessment

The OECD's 2025 assessment found that "although AI use is increasing, AI use in government has not yet made a transformative impact."[^2]

**Key Challenges Identified**:
1. Skills shortages in government
2. Outdated legacy systems
3. Difficulties in data sharing
4. Financial constraints
5. Gap between strategy and implementation

### Overall Effectiveness Rating

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Normative Frameworks | 7/10 | Strong convergence, broad participation |
| Technical Standards | 6/10 | Good interoperability, implementation lags |
| Enforcement & Compliance | 2/10 | Mostly voluntary, weak monitoring |
| Speed & Adaptability | 4/10 | Improving, but still lags technology |
| Practical Impact | 3/10 | Limited transformation of actual practices |
| **Composite** | **4.4/10** | **Moderate-Low Effectiveness** |

**Trajectory**: Normative convergence and governance infrastructure are improving, but the implementation gap remains the critical weakness.

## 6. Technology Transfer to Authoritarian Regimes

### China-Russia AI Cooperation

#### Sino-Russian Innovation Dialogue

**Established**: 2017 (annual since then, according to some sources)

**Participants**: China's Ministry of Science and Technology + Russia's Ministry of Economic Development

**2019-2024 Work Plan**: Reportedly combines "China's industry, capital and market with the resources, technology and talents of Russia"

#### Russia's AI Dependence on China

**LLM Sources**: According to some analysts, a significant share of LLMs available globally originate from China, though precise figures vary by methodology

**Russia's AI Funding**: Reportedly 5.2 billion rubles (approximately \$57 million) in 2024

**US Comparison**: The US government has allocated substantially more to AI than Russia in recent years, though specific multiples depend on what programs are counted

**Implication**: Russia is increasingly dependent on Chinese AI models and technology given funding constraints and Western sanctions

### Global Spread of Chinese Surveillance AI

| Metric | Value | Trend | Note |
|--------|-------|-------|------|
| **Countries with PRC surveillance** | 80+ countries (reported) | Expanding via Digital Silk Road | Per Carnegie Endowment research |
| **Global surveillance camera market share** | ≈34% (Hikvision + Dahua, reported) | Dominant | Per Atlantic Council analysis |
| **AI export country reach** | Reportedly ≈2x US reach | Growing | Per Perth USAsia Centre research |
| **Smart city projects** | Hundreds in developing countries (estimated) | Accelerating | Per multiple researchers |

#### Geographic Reach

Researchers at the Carnegie Endowment for International Peace have reported that **80+ countries** have received PRC-sourced AI-for-surveillance solutions. Recipients reportedly include both authoritarian regimes and democracies—among them Malaysia, Vietnam, Thailand, Serbia, Kenya, Pakistan, Laos, and countries throughout the Middle East.

#### Market Dominance in Surveillance

The Atlantic Council has reported that **Hikvision and Dahua combined** hold approximately **34% of the global surveillance camera market** (based on data through 2024). These firms supply integrated systems that reportedly combine facial recognition, predictive policing, and real-time geographic monitoring.

The Australian Strategic Policy Institute (ASPI) has noted that "AI has become the backbone of a far more pervasive and predictive form of authoritarian control," though this characterization reflects analytical judgment rather than a directly measured claim.

#### Dual-Use Technology Proliferation

Researchers at the Perth USAsia Centre have reported that China exports AI technology to nearly **twice as many countries as the United States**, though this figure depends on methodology and definitions of "AI export."

Academic research suggests Chinese surveillance technology exports are disproportionately directed at autocracies and weak democracies, and that such governments are more likely to import during periods of domestic unrest.

The National Endowment for Democracy has argued that China's AI advances, including the development of models such as DeepSeek, "will boost the government's surveillance capabilities, including overseas," framing this as part of a broader effort to spread techno-authoritarian practices.

### Weaponization of AI for Disinformation

#### State Actors Using Generative AI

According to some analysts and researchers, state actors including **Iran, Russia, and Venezuela** have been "purposefully experimenting with and weaponizing generative AI to manipulate the information space and undermine democracy."

During the **2024 US election cycle**, AI-generated fake images and deepfakes were widely reported to have proliferated across social media platforms, though attribution of specific campaigns to state actors varied.

The accessibility of Chinese-origin open-source AI models has raised concerns among researchers that such tools "can be made to adhere to the standards of authoritarian regimes," enabling disinformation at scale.

### Technology Transfer Mechanisms

#### Commercial Exports

- Surveillance cameras and software
- Facial recognition systems
- Smart city infrastructure packages

#### Government-to-Government

- Belt and Road Initiative technology components
- Bilateral cooperation agreements
- Training programs for foreign officials

#### Open Source & Indirect

- Open-source AI models usable by any regime
- Academic collaborations
- Commercial AI services available globally

### Democratic Responses

#### Export Controls & Investment Screening

**The US and Australia have been leading coordination on**:
- Export controls for sensitive AI technologies
- Investment screening mechanisms
- Restrictions on collaboration with military-linked Chinese institutions

**Challenge**: Dual-use technologies with legitimate commercial applications are inherently difficult to control through export restrictions alone.

#### EU AI Act (2024)

**Approved**: The European Parliament passed the EU AI Act in March 2024, following political agreement in December 2023.

**Scope**: The Act aims to "protect fundamental rights, democracy, the rule of law and environmental sustainability" through a risk-based regulatory framework.

**Approach**: Stricter requirements apply to high-risk AI applications, with some categories prohibited outright.

**Impact on exports**: Sets a baseline standard for AI systems sold into the EU market, with potential extraterritorial effects.

#### Multilateral Coordination

In **April 2024**, a workshop co-hosted by the Hoover Institution, Stanford Global Digital Policy Incubator, and the National Endowment for Democracy focused on efforts to "map the expanding frontiers of digital authoritarianism" and "discuss the diffusion of authoritarian technologies."

### Scale of Technology Transfer

#### Surveillance Technology

**Conservative Estimate**: 80+ countries reportedly host PRC-linked AI surveillance deployments (per Carnegie Endowment)

**Market Value**: Hikvision and Dahua represent billions in annual surveillance equipment sales globally

#### AI Models & Services

**Accessibility**: Chinese LLMs and AI services are available to most countries globally with few formal restrictions

**Control**: Minimal formal controls currently apply to the export of general-purpose AI models

#### Smart City Projects

Hundreds of projects in developing countries reportedly incorporate Chinese AI surveillance and management systems, though comprehensive audited counts are not publicly available.

### Assessment

**Current Risk Level**: **High**

**Evidence**:
- Reported dominant market share in surveillance technology (approximately 34%, per Atlantic Council)
- Deployment reportedly spanning 80+ countries (per Carnegie Endowment)
- Documented experimentation by Iran, Russia, and Venezuela with AI-enabled disinformation
- Rapid proliferation of generative AI capabilities with limited export controls
- Limited effective mechanisms to restrict dual-use technology exports

**Trend**: **Accelerating**

Proliferation appears to be outpacing the development of effective control mechanisms. Open-source AI development further complicates restriction efforts, as models once released cannot be recalled.

## Key Takeaways

### Competition vs. Cooperation Balance

**Competition Indicators**: Dominant
- 12:1 US private investment advantage, but China government investment competitive
- Military AI spending growing 15-20%+ annually
- Autonomous weapons proliferation accelerating
- Technology transfer to rivals ongoing

**Cooperation Indicators**: Present but Weak
- 87% convergence across governance frameworks
- 47 countries signed onto OECD principles
- Multiple bilateral safety agreements
- BUT: mostly non-binding, weak enforcement, significant implementation gap

### Critical Vulnerabilities

1. **US Talent Dependence**: 38% of top US AI researchers from China
2. **Declining Mobility**: Global talent staying home more (13 percentage point drop)
3. **Governance-Capability Gap**: 53 percentage point gap between AI adoption and governance maturity
4. **Surveillance Proliferation**: Chinese AI surveillance in 80+ countries
5. **Military AI Testing**: Ukraine conflict accelerating autonomous weapons development

### Trajectories

**Near-term (1-3 years)**:
- US maintains overall capability lead but gap narrows
- Military AI spending continues rapid growth
- Governance frameworks proliferate but remain weak
- Technology transfer to authoritarian regimes continues

**Medium-term (3-7 years)**:
- China could achieve parity in specific AI domains
- Autonomous weapons become standard military capability
- International governance either strengthens significantly or fragments
- Competition vs. cooperation balance likely determines AI risk landscape

## Limitations & Data Quality

### Strengths
- Investment data from multiple authoritative sources (KPMG, EY, Stanford)
- Talent data from rigorous academic tracking (MacroPolo)
- Government spending from official budgets where available
- Think tank analysis from CSET, RAND, Carnegie

### Limitations
1. **China data opacity**: Actual military spending and government AI investment likely understated by 40-90%
2. **Talent attribution**: Based on undergraduate institution, may not perfectly reflect current national affiliation
3. **Technology transfer**: Difficult to quantify; much occurs through commercial channels
4. **Governance effectiveness**: Largely qualitative assessment, hard to measure concrete impact
5. **Rapid change**: AI landscape evolving faster than data collection cycles

### Update Frequency
- Investment data: Quarterly to annual
- Talent flows: Major update every 3 years (MacroPolo)
- Military spending: Annual budget cycles
- Governance: Ongoing policy tracking, major reports annual
- Technology proliferation: Ad hoc reporting, no systematic tracking
