## Overview

This page tracks the economic and labor market dimensions of AI development, including investment flows, market valuations, employment impacts, and productivity gains. These metrics help assess AI's integration into the economy and its effects on work and value creation.

## Labor Market Impact

### Jobs Displaced by AI

**Current Displacement (2024)**
- **14% of all workers** have reportedly already experienced AI-related displacement, with higher rates among younger and mid-career workers in tech and creative fields, according to some surveys.[^1]
- **12,700 jobs** were estimated to have been lost directly to AI in 2024, representing approximately 0.1% of all layoffs tracked that year, according to ITIF analysis.[^2]
- **Goldman Sachs** estimated that roughly 2.5% of US employment could be at risk under moderate AI adoption scenarios, rising to 6–7% under wide adoption.[^3]

**Projected Displacement (2030)**
- **McKinsey** has projected that activities accounting for approximately 30% of hours worked in the US economy could be automated by 2030.[^4]
- Separately, McKinsey estimates that up to 40% of workers in highly automatable roles may need to shift occupations, with roughly 57% of work hours technically automatable using current or near-term technology.[^4]

**Bureau of Labor Statistics Projections (2023–2033)**
- **Bank tellers**: projected −15% employment change, corresponding to an estimated 51,400 fewer positions.[^5]
- **Cashiers**: projected −11%, corresponding to an estimated 353,100 fewer positions.[^5]
- Research from the St. Louis Fed has found that unemployment increases are correlated with higher AI exposure scores; computer and mathematical occupations show elevated exposure (around 80% on some exposure indices).[^6]

**High-Risk Sectors**
- Office support, customer service, and food service are widely cited as facing near-term automation pressure.
- Manufacturing is projected by some analysts to see up to 30% of roles automatable by the mid-2030s.
- Financial services is considered to face shorter-term vulnerability due to high proportions of routine cognitive tasks.

**Low-Risk Sectors**
- Construction and skilled trades, personal care services, and most healthcare professional roles are generally considered lower risk.
- Healthcare professionals and STEM roles are projected by some analyses to see 17–30% employment growth over the same horizon, according to McKinsey reports.[^4]

**Gender Disparities**
- According to some analyses, approximately **79% of employed women** in the US work in roles considered at high risk of automation, compared with roughly 58% of men.[^1]
- In high-income nations, an estimated **9.6% of women's jobs** are at the highest automation risk, versus approximately **3.2% for men**, according to reported survey data.[^1]

**Data Quality**: Moderate. Displacement projections vary widely across studies, with estimates ranging from roughly 9% to 47% of jobs. Task-based approaches (as used by the OECD) tend to produce lower risk estimates than occupation-based methods (as in Frey & Osborne 2013).

### Jobs Created by AI

**Current Job Creation (2024)**
- ITIF estimated that AI directly created approximately **119,900 jobs** in 2024: around 8,900 in AI model development and operations (ML engineers, data scientists) and over 110,000 in construction tied to AI-driven data center buildout.[^2]
- On a net basis, this implies an estimated **+107,200 jobs** when set against the 12,700 direct losses estimated for the same year.[^2]

**Job Market Growth (Q1 2025)**
- One industry analysis reported **35,445 AI-related open positions** in the US in Q1 2025, representing a 25.2% increase from Q1 2024 and an 8.8% increase from Q4 2024.[^7]
- AI job postings reportedly more than doubled between 2023 and 2024, with a 56% increase in AI job share in 2025 compared to 2024, according to the same analysis.[^7]

**Long-term Projections**
- The **World Economic Forum** has projected that AI and automation could generate up to **97 million new roles** globally by 2025, though this figure carries substantial uncertainty.[^8]
- **McKinsey** has estimated 20–50 million new AI-related jobs globally by 2030, contingent on adoption speed and policy environment.[^4]
- The **US Bureau of Labor Statistics** projects computer and information research scientists will grow **+23%** from 2022 to 2032.[^5]

**Data Science & ML Engineer Outlook**
- BLS projects data science occupations will grow **+36%** from 2023 to 2033, and operations research analysts **+23%** over the same period.[^5]
- Estimates of over 500,000 ML engineering positions available worldwide in 2025 have been reported by some industry sources, though this figure is difficult to verify independently.[^7]

**Data Quality**: Good for BLS occupational projections and job-posting counts. Long-term aggregate forecasts (e.g., 97 million jobs) are highly uncertain and should be treated as illustrative scenarios rather than predictions.

## Productivity & Economic Impact

### AI Productivity Gains

**McKinsey Global Institute Estimates**

According to McKinsey Global Institute's report on the economic potential of generative AI, the technology could add an estimated **\$2.6–4.4 trillion annually** across use cases analyzed — a figure some summaries report with a wider range of \$1.6–4.4 trillion when broader automation is included.[^1] This is roughly equivalent to adding an economy the size of the United Kingdom each year. McKinsey also projects **labor productivity growth of 0.1–0.6% annually** through 2040, rising to **0.2–3.3 percentage points** when combined with all automation technologies.[^1]

**Value Concentration**

McKinsey estimates that approximately **75% of generative AI's total value** would be concentrated in four functional areas: customer operations, marketing and sales, software engineering, and R&D.[^1]

**Alternative Estimates**

Economist Daron Acemoglu has offered more conservative projections, reportedly estimating only around **0.07% annual productivity growth** attributable to AI over the next decade, translating to roughly **0.9–1.8% cumulative GDP growth** over ten years.[^2] The Penn Wharton Budget Model projects a **1.5% productivity and GDP increase by 2035**, rising to **3% by 2055** and **3.7% by 2075**.[^3]

**Current Adoption Gap**

Despite high executive awareness, reportedly **only 1% of organizations** have achieved mature AI deployment at scale — suggesting the productivity gains above reflect long-run potential rather than near-term reality.[^2]

> **Data Quality Note**: Estimates carry wide uncertainty ranges reflecting genuine unknowns about adoption speed, task complementarity, and how productivity gains translate to GDP.

### Fortune 500 & Enterprise Adoption

- **92% of Fortune 500** companies reportedly use ChatGPT.[^1]
- **70% of Fortune 500** companies reportedly use both ChatGPT and Microsoft Copilot.[^1]
- **78% of all organizations** use AI in at least one business function (up from 55% in 2023), according to McKinsey.[^2]
- **71% of organizations** use generative AI regularly (up from 33% in 2023), according to McKinsey.[^2]

*Data quality: High for large-company survey data. Self-reported "adoption" definitions vary across surveys.*

- An estimated **74% still struggle to scale** AI despite regular use.[^3]

**By Company Revenue Size**

- **High performers**: approximately 75% are scaling or have scaled AI, versus 33% of other organizations.[^2]
- **Under \$100M revenue**: only about 29% have reached the scaling phase.[^2]

**Regional Adoption**

- **North America**: approximately 82% adoption.[^4]

- **India**: approximately 59% integration, cited as a global leader.[^4]
- **UAE**: approximately 58%.[^4]

## Cost Savings & ROI

### Enterprise Cost Savings

**Aggregate Impact**

According to McKinsey's 2024 State of AI report, leading companies attribute more than 10% of EBIT to generative AI.[^1] Self-reported figures from industry surveys suggest mid-market enterprises save approximately \$1.4 million annually, though these estimates are subject to selection bias and should be treated with caution.[^2] Reported ROI multiples of around 3.2x within 18 months and operational cost reductions of ~35% over the same period circulate widely in vendor and consulting literature but lack independent verification.[^2]

**McKinsey Survey Results**

McKinsey's 2024 survey found that 42% of organizations report cost reductions attributable to AI (including generative AI), and 59% report revenue increases — representing roughly a 10-percentage-point rise in cost-reduction reports compared with the prior year.[^1]

**By Industry (ROI Multiples)**

Vendor analyses reportedly place financial services at 4–6x ROI (attributed to data-rich environments), manufacturing at 3–5x (predictive maintenance and quality control), and healthcare/professional services at 2.5–4x.[^3] These figures are self-reported and unaudited; treat them as indicative rather than definitive.

**Manufacturing-Specific**
Early AI adopters in manufacturing reportedly achieve 15–25% operational cost recovery and 30–50% defect reduction, according to industry sources.[^4] One frequently cited claim attributes a 73% within-18-months ROI rate to Gartner research, though this could not be independently verified and should be treated as approximate.

**Time Savings**

Survey data suggests workers save an average of roughly one hour per day through AI assistance, with sector variation: energy/utilities workers reportedly save ~75 minutes daily and manufacturing workers ~62 minutes.[^5] Lumen Technologies has publicly stated its sellers recover approximately four hours weekly using AI tools.[^5]

**Implementation Costs vs. Returns**

Implementation costs vary widely: deploying an off-the-shelf model is estimated at roughly \$2 million, customizing an existing model at ≈\$10 million, and building from scratch at ≈\$200 million.[^6] Accenture has argued that strategic enterprise scaling delivers approximately 3x the returns of siloed proofs-of-concept, though this figure comes from Accenture's own research.[^6]

**Reality Check**

A late-2024 BCG analysis found that only 4% of companies have deployed "cutting-edge" AI capabilities enterprise-wide, 22% are beginning to realize substantial gains, and 74% have yet to demonstrate tangible value despite significant investment.[^7]

> **Data Quality Note:** Most ROI figures in this section are self-reported by vendors, consultants, or survey respondents. Early adopters are likely overrepresented, and independent audits of claimed savings remain rare.

## Automation Risk Scores

### Risk by Occupation Type

**OECD Analysis**

The OECD has produced two widely cited estimates using different methodologies. An earlier task-based analysis found that approximately 9% of jobs across 21 OECD countries are highly automatable, defined as more than 25 out of 100 skills or abilities being easily automatable.[^1] A subsequent OECD estimate, using updated methods, placed 27% of jobs at high automation risk on average across OECD member countries.[^2]

**Cross-Country Variation**

Automation risk varies substantially across countries. Korea reportedly shows among the lowest shares at around 6% of jobs at high risk, while Austria is cited at approximately 12%.[^3] The United States figure of roughly 9% under the task-based OECD approach contrasts sharply with the earlier 47% estimate produced by Frey and Osborne (2013) using an occupation-based approach — a difference driven largely by methodology, not underlying labor-market conditions.[^4]

**US-Specific Risk Distribution**

A Minnesota Department of Employment and Economic Development (DEED) study found that approximately 12.6% of US workers — roughly 19.2 million people — face high or very high automation risk, while around 39% face little or no risk, with the remainder at slight or moderate risk.[^5]

**Federal Reserve Analysis**

According to St. Louis Fed research covering 2022–2025, occupations with higher AI exposure reportedly experienced larger unemployment increases, with a correlation coefficient of approximately 0.47 between AI exposure scores and unemployment growth. Computer and mathematical occupations, which score around 80% on AI exposure measures, showed some of the steepest unemployment rises in that period.[^6]

### High-Risk Occupations

BLS employment projections identify several declining occupations linked to automation, including:[^7]

- Bank tellers (projected −15% by 2033)
- Cashiers (projected −11% by 2033)
- Customer service representatives
- Nonmedical secretaries and administrative assistants
- Credit authorizers and procurement clerks

Broader analyses also flag computer programmers, accountants, auditors, legal assistants, and technical writers as facing elevated automation exposure.[^8]

### Low-Risk Occupations

BLS projections show strong growth in categories less susceptible to automation:[^9]

- Healthcare professionals (projected +17–30% growth)
- Personal financial advisors (projected +17.1% growth, 2023–2033)
- STEM professionals
- Construction and skilled trades
- Personal services (food service workers, medical assistants, cleaners)

Database administrators are noted as a case where AI augments rather than replaces demand.

### Historical Employment Growth Despite Automation

OECD research examining within-country employment trends found that jobs classified as high automation risk still saw roughly 6% employment growth over the 2012–recent period, compared with 18% growth for low-risk jobs.[^10] However, the OECD also found that low-educated workers are increasingly concentrated in high-risk occupations, raising distributional concerns.[^11]

### Methodological Note

Task-based approaches (as used by the OECD) yield substantially lower automation risk estimates than occupation-based approaches (as used by Frey and Osborne). Because workers within the same occupation often perform different mixes of tasks, treating the entire occupation as automatable overstates risk. Task-level analysis is generally considered the more accurate framework.[^12]

**Data Quality**: Moderate to high. Methodological differences drive wide variation in headline figures (9% vs. 47%). Task-based estimates are considered more reliable for policy purposes.

## GDP & Macroeconomic Indicators

### GDP Per Capita Growth & AI Contribution

**IMF Perspective (2024)**
- **Baseline forecast**: Global growth at approximately 3.1% over the medium term — reportedly among the lowest projections in decades[^1]
- **Key driver of slowdown**: Widespread decline in total factor productivity[^1]
- **Contributing factors**: Capital and labor misallocation, demographic pressures, and reduced private investment[^1]
- **AI as potential solution**: The IMF has described AI as offering what some analysts characterize as the best near-term opportunity to ease supply-side constraints and reverse productivity decline[^1]

**Potential AI Impact**
- Could produce a major sustained surge in productivity, according to some IMF analyses, reversing the downward trend in growth[^2]
- Without significant policy action or technological advances, medium-term growth is projected to remain below pre-pandemic levels[^2]

**Current GDP Per Capita Trends**
- Substantial inequality persists between countries[^3]
- Poorest countries: average incomes under approximately \$1,000 per year[^3]
- High-income countries: average incomes exceeding \$50,000 per year — more than 50 times higher[^3]

**McKinsey GDP Growth Estimates**
- **Generative AI impact**: According to McKinsey research, generative AI could add the equivalent of 1.5–3.4 percentage points to average annual GDP growth in advanced economies over the next decade[^4]
- **Combined automation**: Broader automation scenarios project a 0.2–3.3 percentage point annual productivity boost[^4]

**Data Quality**: Low to moderate. AI-specific GDP contribution is difficult to isolate. Estimates are based on economic modeling rather than observed data.

### Labor Force Participation Rate

**Overall US Trends (BLS 2024–2034 Projections)**
- **Total employment**: Projected at approximately 175.2 million by 2034[^5]
- **Growth rate**: +3.1% (2024–2034), markedly slower than the 13.0% growth recorded over 2014–2024[^5]
- **Net new jobs**: Approximately 5.2 million over the decade[^5]
- **Main driver**: Healthcare and social assistance sector[^5]

**Participation Rate Trends**
- **Overall**: A continual decline in the labor force participation rate is projected[^6]
- **Primary cause**: Demographic shifts, as the population ages and older individuals participate in the labor force at lower rates[^6]
- **Prime-age participation**: Further decline projected through 2034, driven largely by men's participation (which has been declining for decades); women's rate is relatively more stable[^6]

**Sector-Specific Impacts**

| Sector | Direction | Projected Change |
|---|---|---|
| Retail trade | Declining | Among highest job losses |
| Manufacturing | Declining | Automation-driven |
| Mining / oil & gas | Declining | Productivity gains from robotics |
| Healthcare professionals | Growing | +17–30% |
| Personal financial advisors | Growing | +17.1% |
| STEM roles | Growing | Strong demand |

*Sources: BLS Industry Projections[^7] and BLS AI Impacts analysis[^8]*

**AI's Mixed Effect**
- Some occupations face productivity-driven growth limits — for example, technical writers and customer service representatives[^8]
- Others see AI as a supportive tool that increases overall demand for the role, including database administrators and architects or engineers[^8]
- Overall, structural change and workforce disruption are expected to continue for decades[^8]

**Data Quality**: High for BLS demographic projections. AI-specific impact on participation rates remains difficult to isolate from broader economic and demographic trends.

## Key Uncertainties & Data Gaps

### Major Uncertainties

1. **Productivity Translation**: Wide variance in estimates (0.07% to 3.4% GDP growth)
2. **Adoption Speed**: Only 1-4% of companies at mature deployment despite high awareness
3. **Job Displacement Timeline**: Projections range from 9% to 47% of jobs at risk
4. **ROI Realization**: 74% of companies yet to show tangible value despite investment
5. **Geographic Distribution**: Will AI benefits concentrate or distribute globally?

### Data Limitations

1. **Private company data**: Valuations based on funding rounds, not observable metrics
2. **Corporate R&D**: Private AI spending likely underreported
3. **Job quality**: Metrics track quantity but not compensation/conditions of new jobs
4. **Indirect effects**: Spillover impacts on non-AI sectors difficult to measure
5. **Definitional issues**: What counts as "AI" varies across studies and companies

### Update Frequency

- **Investment data**: Quarterly (VC), Annual (corporate capex)
- **Employment data**: Monthly (BLS), Annual (projections)
- **Salary data**: Annual (BLS), Continuous (Glassdoor, Levels.fyi)
- **Market size**: Annual estimates, Semi-annual updates
- **Productivity/GDP**: Quarterly (GDP), Annual (productivity estimates)
